A Critical Professional Resource for Early Childhood Educators

Early childhood education is inherently political, and I approach this portfolio from that conviction. Supporting children facing disadvantage, displacement, trauma, or systemic inequity requires me to understand how these conditions are produced through policy, institutions, and historical power, and to respond with ethical rigour and purposeful advocacy. This is consistent with the Early Childhood Australia Code of Ethics (Early Childhood Australia [ECA], 2016), which places ethical, respectful, and socially responsible practice at the centre of professional identity.

Theoretical Foundations

This portfolio draws on ecological and critical sociological perspectives to make structural inequality visible. Bronfenbrenner’s bioecological theory is foundational because it demonstrates that children develop within nested, interconnected systems (microsystem, mesosystem, exosystem, macrosystem, and chronosystem) and that early childhood settings play a critical mesosystem role, linking children’s home lives with the broader social world (Bronfenbrenner, 1979, as cited in Murray & Harrison, 2022, p. 216). This model anchors the portfolio because it frames every context explored (economic, social, cultural, health, and crisis) as part of a layered system that shapes children’s development. Critical theory extends this by questioning whose experiences are centred and how systems may reproduce inequality while appearing supportive (Grace & Menzie, 2022, p. 287).

Attachment theory reinforces that early childhood practice must remain relational at its core. When attachment is disrupted through separation, trauma, or caregiver unavailability, safe and emotionally available educators can become important sources of stability (Bowlby, 1969, as cited in Cashmore et al., 2022). Strengths-based and rights-based practice also anchors every section of this portfolio. As Woodrow et al. (2022) caution, deficit approaches that define families by the challenges they face and “position them as weak and in need of rescue by professionals” (p. 363) can reproduce the harm they claim to address.

1. Understanding the Context

Poverty, housing stress, and persistent financial hardship are not the product of individual failure. They are structural outcomes shaped by low wages, insecure employment, rising housing costs, and policy frameworks that manage rather than resolve inequality. In Australia, 13.4% of the population, including approximately 761,000 children, live below the poverty line (ACOSS & UNSW Sydney, 2023). The persistence of child poverty in one of the world’s wealthiest nations reflects structural inaction, not personal misfortune.

Bronfenbrenner’s bioecological theory demonstrates that economic hardship operates across all interconnected systems simultaneously. Financial stress shapes daily routines, parental emotional availability, and access to services, all of which are largely beyond individual family control (Bronfenbrenner, 1979, as cited in Murray & Harrison, 2022, p. 216). Bourdieu’s concept of capital deepens this analysis: inequality is reproduced not only through income but through unequal access to knowledge, networks, and institutional power. For families with fewer economic resources, this means reduced access to the cultural and social capital that more advantaged families draw on to access educational systems, compounding disadvantage across generations (Bourdieu, 1986, as cited in Grace & Baird, 2022, p. 243).

Families experiencing hardship are not homogeneous. Sole-parent families, First Nations families, families living with disability, newly arrived communities, and those in rural or remote areas encounter overlapping structural barriers. Grace and Baird (2022) demonstrate that children in socio-economically disadvantaged communities are almost three times more likely to begin school developmentally vulnerable (p. 248). As a teacher, I must resist deficit explanations and recognise economic hardship as a structural context that shapes children’s opportunities in unequal ways.

The conditions of economic hardship in Australia are actively changing. Rising housing costs, sustained inflation, and the spread of insecure, casualised, and gig-economy employment mean that financial stress now reaches families who previously maintained economic stability. ACOSS and UNSW Sydney (2023) document a growing number of working families who cannot cover the difference between wages and the real costs of housing, childcare, and essential goods. As a teacher, this means I cannot identify economic hardship by appearance alone; the context is dynamic, increasingly diverse, and not easily categorised.

2. Impact on Children and Families

“22% of Australian children begin school developmentally vulnerable”
Baker et al., 2022, p. 355

Impact on Children

  • Economic hardship limits access to stable housing, nutritious food, healthcare, and quality learning resources, each a foundational condition for healthy development.
  • Baker et al. (2022) note that approximately 22% of Australian children begin school developmentally vulnerable, with children from disadvantaged communities significantly over-represented (p. 355).
  • Hardship may appear as fatigue, reduced concentration, irregular attendance, or difficulty participating in everyday learning. These outcomes must never be interpreted as evidence of poor parenting or low family commitment.
  • High-quality early childhood settings can buffer the effects of family stress, though only when genuinely accessible and free from stigma (Murray & Harrison, 2022, p. 219).

Impact on Families

  • Poverty often produces cognitive and emotional overload for caregivers managing multiple systems with few resources.
  • Apparent low engagement frequently reflects transport barriers, irregular work schedules, digital exclusion, or the daily pressure of meeting basic needs.
  • For sole-parent families, these pressures fall on one caregiver alone; for newly arrived families, unfamiliarity with systems adds a further layer.
  • My role is not to judge this absence but to ask what barriers are shaping participation and how I can respond more flexibly and respectfully.

3. Social Policy and Australian Responses

🇦🇺 National

Child Care Subsidy, 2023 Amendments

The Activity Test was removed for vulnerable families. Yet many families remain unable to cover the difference between the subsidy and actual care costs, and the tension between early childhood education as a public good and as a market commodity remains unresolved (Australian Government Department of Education, 2023).

🇦🇺 National

Family Tax Benefit (Parts A and B)

Provides supplementary income support for eligible families. ACOSS (2023) argues these payments have not kept pace with the cost of living, meaning hardship may be reduced without being structurally resolved.

🇦🇺 National

Universal Access to Early Childhood Education

The commitment to 15 hours of quality preschool for all children in the year before school has expanded participation, but implementation quality varies significantly across communities (Australian Government, 2023). The children who would benefit most remain the least likely to attend.

📍 State – Queensland

Homes for Queenslanders Plan 2023–2027

Commits substantial investment to social and affordable housing (Queensland Government, 2023a). Housing demand continues to exceed supply, and the timeline to 2046 means children experiencing housing stress today will have aged out of early childhood services before this commitment is realised.

Baker et al. (2022) observe that policy increasingly promotes “early intervention over crisis management” (p. 355), while Woodrow et al. (2022) caution that fragmented service systems continue to place the burden of coordination on the families least resourced to manage it (p. 366). If support remains difficult to access, equity remains a promise rather than a lived reality.

4. Evidence-Based Strategies for Practice

1

Build trust through relationship-based practice.

Murray and Harrison (2022) identify family partnership built on agency and belonging as central to quality engagement (p. 229).

In practice: For First Nations families with well-founded reasons to distrust institutions, relationship must be built before participation can be expected. Greeting every family by name and following up individually by phone, rather than relying on group notifications, communicates genuine regard.

2

Proactively reduce participation barriers.

Economic exclusion is structural; responses must be too.

In practice: Auditing all service communications for digital exclusion, eliminating hidden costs, and ensuring no child is visibly excluded due to family finances are baseline obligations (Woodrow et al., 2022, p. 367). For newly arrived families unfamiliar with the subsidy system, and for families living with disability facing transport barriers, this audit must extend to physical accessibility and plain-language communication.

3

Create emotionally predictable, regulation-supportive environments.

Grace et al. (2022) note that chronic adversity can affect attention and impulse regulation (p. 35).

In practice: For children in sole-parent households managing financial stress with no second caregiver to absorb the pressure, or for children newly relocated from rural and remote areas, consistent routines and personalised arrival rituals may be the most stabilising part of their day.

4

Use strengths-based communication.

Woodrow et al. (2022) describe strengths-based practice as focusing on what is working and how those strengths can address challenges (p. 364).

In practice: For First Nations families whose knowledge has been historically devalued, and for families living with disability who frequently encounter deficit framing, this reorientation is not a courtesy; it is an act of professional justice.

5

Facilitate warm and timely intersectoral referral.

Baker et al. (2022) argue children and families are best supported when services are connected rather than fragmented (p. 342).

In practice: For rural and remote families with few local services, knowing which telehealth and outreach options are available is a professional obligation. For newly arrived families, a referral made with the family rather than for them is the difference between genuine support and a gesture. The EYLF V2.0 (AGDE, 2022) positions collaborative partnerships as a core professional responsibility.

1. Understanding the Context

Social isolation, family separation, and out-of-home care (OOHC) are not simply relational difficulties. They are conditions shaped by structural disadvantage, historical policy decisions, and systems that have repeatedly failed the families most in need of support. Bronfenbrenner's bioecological theory helps explain this clearly: children develop within nested, interconnected systems, and early childhood settings occupy the mesosystem, where wellbeing is shaped not only by what happens within educational settings, but by the quality of relationships between family and service (Bronfenbrenner, 1979, as cited in Murray & Harrison, 2022, p. 225). When those relationships are disrupted through separation, placement instability, or community disconnection, children's capacity to engage in learning is directly affected.

Attachment theory provides an important additional lens because disruptions to primary relationships can profoundly affect children's sense of safety, trust, and emotional regulation (Bowlby, 1969, as cited in Cashmore et al., 2022). For children experiencing placement instability or major family change, the early childhood educator may become one of the most consistent adults in their week. Relationship-building, therefore, cannot be treated as secondary to learning; it is one of the conditions that make learning possible.

As of June 2020, approximately 46,000 children were in OOHC nationally (Cashmore et al., 2022, p. 270). Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children were 11 times more likely to be in OOHC, a figure that reflects colonisation, intergenerational trauma, and systemic bias rather than family failure (Grace & Menzie, 2022, p. 288). Social disruption, however, extends beyond OOHC. Children from same-sex parent families may encounter heteronormative assumptions embedded in enrolment forms and classroom language. Adoptive families may face questions of identity, origins, and disclosure that require particular sensitivity, as children can carry complex narratives about early separation that are not always visible.

The 11x over-representation of Indigenous children in out-of-home care is not a measure of family failure. It is a measure of systemic racism, structural disadvantage, and the enduring legacy of policies designed to break First Nations families.

2. Impact on Children and Families

Cashmore et al., 2022, p. 270

Impact on Children

  • Social isolation, separation, and care disruption affect emotional security, identity development, peer relationships, and learning in cumulative ways.
  • Repeated changes of carers, schools, and workers disrupt educational continuity and children's capacity to maintain a coherent sense of self (Cashmore et al., 2022, p. 275).
  • Children may present through withdrawal, anxiety, dysregulation, or persistently testing relational limits (adaptive responses to relational uncertainty, not character flaws requiring correction).
  • The EYLF V2.0 (AGDE, 2022) recognises belonging as a condition actively created through intentional practice.

Impact on Families

  • These contexts often involve grief, legal complexity, service involvement, and emotional exhaustion for carers navigating multiple systems simultaneously.
  • Apparent low engagement frequently reflects the burden of crisis management rather than disengagement from the child's needs.
  • Woodrow et al. (2022) caution that deficit responses discount families' knowledge of their own situations (p. 366).
  • For same-sex parent families and adoptive families, institutional assumptions embedded in forms and communications add their own layer of invisible complexity.
  • My role is not to judge this complexity but to ask what is shaping participation and how the service can respond with greater flexibility, safety, and genuine respect.

3. Social Policy and Australian Responses

🇦🇺 National

Safe and Supported: National Framework 2021–2031

Promotes earlier family support. Cashmore et al. (2022) argue that child protection responses have historically remained reactive and crisis-oriented, focused on investigation after harm rather than prevention before it (p. 277). This framework signals a shift, but has not yet fundamentally altered that pattern (Department of Social Services, 2021).

🇦🇺 National

Family Law Amendment Act 2023

Strengthens protections for children's safety in contested parenting matters, replacing the presumption of equal shared responsibility with a framework centring child safety. Unequal access to legal representation means the most vulnerable families may not fully benefit from these protections.

📍 State – Queensland

Child Protection Act 1999 and Reform Roadmap 2023–2026

Guides statutory intervention and OOHC placement. The Reform Roadmap signals a stronger commitment to prevention, but does not yet provide a funded, time-bound response to the structural causes of First Nations over-representation, a significant omission given the 11x disparity (Queensland Government, 2023c).

📍 State – Queensland

Family and Child Connect

Queensland's early intervention service connecting families to community supports before crisis escalates. This non-statutory pathway matters because statutory involvement itself can intensify stress and erode the trust that makes meaningful support possible.

Baker et al. (2022) argue that intersectoral collaboration leads to more holistic outcomes for children and families (p. 346). Policy is meaningful only when it results in support that is relational, timely, and genuinely accessible. It cannot transfer the burden of coordination onto families already managing crisis.

4. Evidence-Based Strategies for Practice

1

Prioritise relational safety through consistency and predictability.

Murray and Harrison (2022) identify close teacher relationships as central to children's adjustment and belonging (p. 225).

In practice: For children adopted following multiple placements, a consistent key educator communicates permanence that may not have been experienced before. For children in same-sex parent families who experience peer exclusion, a reliably warm and accepting educator relationship is a daily corrective to the invalidation they may encounter outside the service.

2

Use inclusive, flexible communication across all caregiving arrangements.

Using neutral language such as "families and carers" is required under the EYLF V2.0 (AGDE, 2022), which positions inclusive communication as a non-negotiable professional responsibility.

In practice: Enrolment forms that ask only for "mother" and "father" immediately signal to same-sex parent families that the service was not designed for them. For adoptive families, forms assuming biological parentage can force disclosure of adoption history before trust has been established.

3

Interpret behaviour as communication, not character.

Woodrow et al. (2022) caution that deficit framing discounts children's and families' knowledge of their own situations (p. 364). Children in foster care or kinship care, particularly those who experienced early institutionalisation or multiple placements, may present with attachment behaviours that are misread as defiance or anxiety disorders.

In practice: Documenting patterns rather than isolated incidents, and asking what behaviour communicates about safety or connection, produces a more accurate professional response.

4

Build belonging through identity-affirming curriculum.

Curriculum and environment communicate whose family is normal and whose is not. The EYLF V2.0 (AGDE, 2022) requires educators to actively create environments in which all children experience belonging. Displays that include same-sex parent families as everyday representation reduce the social isolation these families experience.

In practice: Designing "All About Me" books without sections requiring biological family information prevents adoptive children from having to choose between participating and disclosing what they are not yet ready to share.

5

Facilitate early, warm, and coordinated referral.

Baker et al. (2022) demonstrate that intersectoral collaboration leads to more holistic outcomes (p. 346).

In practice: Adoptive families may benefit from post-adoption attachment support; a warm referral to an adoption-specialist service is far more effective than generic counselling advice. Same-sex parent families experiencing isolation may benefit from referral to LGBTIQ+ family support networks. The EYLF V2.0 (AGDE, 2022) positions collaborative partnerships as a core professional responsibility.

1. Understanding the Context

Children's identities, languages, and sense of belonging are shaped by the cultural worlds they inhabit from birth. Grace and Menzie (2022) draw on Bronfenbrenner's bioecological theory to show that state intervention affecting Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children is produced at the macrosystem level and permeates every environment in which they live and learn (p. 287). This lens clarifies how colonisation continues to shape the developmental contexts of First Nations children today.

Grace and Menzie (2022) use the concept of cultural hegemony to explain how dominant cultural values marginalise Indigenous families and communities (p. 301). These effects are not historical; they persist through intergenerational trauma and deep mistrust of services. The Bringing Them Home report found that between one in three and one in five Indigenous children were forcibly removed between 1910 and 1970 (HREOC, 1997, as cited in Grace & Menzie, 2022, p. 288). As a teacher, I cannot approach First Nations family engagement as though all families have equal reasons to trust institutions.

For refugee and humanitarian entrant families, Sanagavarapu (2022) shows that transition disrupts relationships, routines, and assumptions, while also noting that families frequently arrive with resilience and hope (pp. 133–144). Multigenerational and joint families from collectivist backgrounds may also experience tensions between extended family structures and services designed around nuclear family norms. Genuine cultural safety requires educators to examine whose knowledge is centred and how practice must be reshaped.

4. Evidence-Based Strategies for Practice

1

Build safe, reciprocal relationships through sustained community engagement.

Grace and Menzie (2022) show that engagement with First Nations families depends on Elders and local leaders and cannot be expedited through goodwill alone (p. 299).

In practice: Attending community events before expecting engagement, and asking to be guided, demonstrate the respect that makes trust possible over time. For refugee and humanitarian families, identifying a bilingual connector before a child's first day means the first relationship at the service is a familiar face, not a form.

2

Create physical environments that communicate cultural safety before any conversation begins.

Grace and Menzie (2022) emphasise that environments signal whether a service is safe long before formal engagement occurs (p. 299).

In practice: For First Nations families, a genuine Welcome to Country and local language at the entrance communicates the service has taken its relationship with Country seriously. For refugee and humanitarian families, multilingual welcome signs signal that the child's home language is valued, not a barrier to manage.

3

Address communication barriers as a systemic, not individual, responsibility.

Sanagavarapu (2022) identifies professional interpreters as essential bridges between refugee families and services (p. 137). Asking children to interpret for their parents undermines both the child's development and the family's dignity.

In practice: For First Nations families, communication must also be free from institutional jargon that has historically been weaponised against them, not merely translated.

4

Embed identity, language, and cultural knowledge in daily curriculum.

Cultural responsiveness must become part of daily pedagogy, not a termly event. The EYLF V2.0 (AGDE, 2022) requires educators to actively create environments where all children experience belonging as an ongoing condition, not an occasional inclusion gesture.

In practice: Using home-language greetings every morning makes linguistic safety a daily act for refugee and humanitarian families whose children are learning in a new language while also settling in. Inviting First Nations families to contribute cultural knowledge on their own terms respects the distinction between cultural sharing and cultural performance. Using the name of the Country the service sits on is the minimum expression of the EYLF V2.0 (AGDE, 2022) requirement for respect for First Nations peoples.

5

Establish genuine, sustained partnerships with community organisations.

Baker et al. (2022) argue that children and families are best supported when services are connected rather than fragmented (p. 342).

In practice: Partnerships with Aboriginal community-controlled organisations ensure referrals to First Nations families carry cultural authority, not just goodwill. Partnerships with multicultural settlement services ensure refugee and humanitarian families are supported by organisations that understand the complexity of navigating new systems, languages, and institutions simultaneously.

1. Understanding the Context

Parental mental illness, substance use, trauma, grief, and chronic family stress are not private failures. They are conditions shaped by structural forces including insecure housing, inadequate income support, intergenerational trauma, and unequal access to mental health care. Woodrow et al. (2022) note that these issues frequently cluster together, creating compounding and intergenerational risk that cannot be addressed through individual-level responses alone (p. 365). Recognising this structural dimension is the difference between genuine understanding and reproducing the stigma that prevents families from seeking help.

When caregivers are emotionally unavailable due to mental illness, grief, or addiction, children's sense of security and capacity for self-regulation are significantly disrupted (Bowlby, 1969, as cited in Cashmore et al., 2022). Perry's neurosequential model explains that repeated exposure to stress activates threat-response systems in ways that reshape how children respond to relationships and learning without consistent relational safety (Perry, 2006, as cited in Woodrow et al., 2022, p. 363). Consistently warm early childhood environments can actively moderate these effects.

One in five Australians experiences a mental health condition annually (ABS, 2022), and 23% of children are estimated to live with a parent experiencing mental illness (AIFS, 2020). Sole-parent families may face compounded pressure because one caregiver manages parenting, employment, and household responsibilities while also managing their own mental health or recovery. Blended and stepfamilies may experience additional strain as children and adults adjust to new household relationships while also responding to emotional instability within the family.

  • Children experiencing trauma are routinely referred for “behaviour support” when what they need is co-regulation and relational safety.
  • Murray and Harrison (2022) demonstrate that warm adult-child relationships can buffer the effects of family stress (p. 219).

Impact on Families

  • For children in sole-parent households with no second caregiver to absorb the pressure, effects are compounded; the same applies in blended families managing a step-parent's mental health.
  • Baker et al. (2022) make clear that children's wellbeing cannot be separated from their caregivers' unresolved needs (p. 355).
  • Apparent disengagement frequently reflects exhaustion, shame, or the burden of managing crisis rather than indifference to their child.
  • Misidentifying trauma-driven behaviour as a conduct concern delays intervention and locates the problem within the child rather than within the system.
  • From my perspective as a teacher, this means I must interpret participation through a structural and relational lens rather than a judgemental one.

4. Evidence-Based Strategies for Practice

1

Create emotionally safe, predictable, and regulation-supportive environments.

Predictability reduces activation of threat-response systems and communicates safety before any words are exchanged (Perry, 2006, as cited in Woodrow et al., 2022, p. 363).

In practice: For a child in a sole-parent household where the morning is disrupted by a parent's depression, a warm and predictable welcome may be the most stabilising moment of their day. For a child in a blended family adjusting to a new household configuration, a consistent arrival ritual offers predictability that home cannot yet guarantee.

2

Co-regulate through warm, attuned relationships.

Murray and Harrison (2022) identify warm adult-child relationships as among the strongest predictors of positive developmental outcomes (p. 219).

In practice: For children in sole-parent households whose only caregiver is managing mental health difficulties, the educator may be the most consistent source of warm co-regulation available. Slowing down, naming the feeling, and sustaining full attention without redirecting to tasks are, in effect, the intervention itself.

3

Interpret behaviour through a trauma-aware, contextual lens.

Woodrow et al. (2022) caution that deficit approaches label families while discounting their knowledge (p. 366). A child in a blended family where a step-parent's mental health episodes create unpredictability may present with vigilance or sudden aggression with no obvious trigger.

In practice: Documenting patterns across time, and asking what behaviour communicates about safety or regulation, produces a more accurate response than labelling it as a conduct problem.

4

Communicate with families non-judgementally and flexibly.

Families managing mental illness, grief, or substance-related challenges often carry significant shame and fear of scrutiny (Woodrow et al., 2022, p. 366).

In practice: For a sole-parent managing their own mental health alongside a child's, brief, warm, strengths-focused communication with multiple contact options reduces barriers significantly. For blended families where the mental health difficulty involves a step-parent rather than the biological parent, asking directly who is the best person to communicate with respects the family's own knowledge of their situation. The EYLF V2.0 (AGDE, 2022) positions active support for family participation (not monitoring from a distance) as a core professional responsibility.

5

Facilitate early, warm, and coordinated referral.

Baker et al. (2022) demonstrate that intersectoral collaboration supports more holistic outcomes (p. 346).

In practice: Sole-parent families managing their own mental health alongside childcare are rarely in a position to navigate referral systems independently; a personal warm referral is far more effective than a pamphlet. For blended families where mental health challenges involve complex stepfamily dynamics, referral to a therapist familiar with those structures acknowledges the specific nature of their situation.

1. Understanding the Context

Crisis and emergency contexts, including family violence, natural disasters, and displacement, are among the most acute challenges affecting children's safety, development, and participation in early childhood education. Their effects often continue long after the immediate crisis, reshaping routines, attachment relationships, housing stability, and access to services across months and years. Bronfenbrenner's bioecological model helps explain this because crisis disrupts children across all interconnected systems simultaneously, from family relationships to community supports and broader state responses (Bronfenbrenner, 1979, as cited in Murray & Harrison, 2022, p. 216).

Perry's neurosequential model provides an essential additional lens: repeated or overwhelming exposure to threat activates children's stress-response systems in ways that, without consistent relational safety, reshape how children respond to relationships and learning (Perry, 2006, as cited in Woodrow et al., 2022, p. 363). These responses are adaptive rather than pathological, and consistently safe early childhood environments can actively moderate their effects.

Crisis does not affect all families equally. Women-led sole-parent households are disproportionately represented among those experiencing family violence, often leaving with children and few financial resources. Foster and kinship care families, and First Nations families in disaster-affected rural communities whose displacement severs connections to Country, face their own compounding pressures. Woodrow et al. (2022) note that adverse childhood experiences multiply where violence, poverty, and social exclusion overlap (p. 363).

Australia's National Plan to End Violence Against Women and Children 2022–2032 is the third such decade-long framework. The first two plans did not achieve their targets. Significant investment in policy has not translated into measurable safety for women and children, and the communities most affected, including First Nations women and children and those who have fled homelessness after escaping violence, continue to face the greatest gaps between policy commitment and lived reality. This plan demands the same scrutiny its predecessors were not given.

DFV Prevention Strategy 2016–2026 and Disaster Management Act 2003

Provide Queensland's coordinated response framework for prevention and emergency planning respectively. The integration of early childhood services into local disaster management plans remains variable across local government areas (Queensland Government, 2016b, 2003).

Baker et al. (2022) caution that despite major policy investment, evidence about what works best, for whom, and under what circumstances remains limited (p. 356). Policy is meaningful only when it becomes timely, coordinated, and accessible support. If families must still face crisis without coordinated support, policy remains more symbolic than actually protective.

4. Evidence-Based Strategies for Practice

1

Prioritise safety, predictability, and calm routines as neurological foundations.

Perry's neurosequential model demonstrates that safety and predictability must be established before developmental or learning goals can be meaningfully pursued (Perry, 2006, as cited in Woodrow et al., 2022, p. 363).

In practice: Using the same warm greeting every arrival, maintaining daily rhythms when disruptions occur, and establishing calm-down spaces as permanent features of the environment are therapeutic acts. For children who have recently fled a domestic violence situation, this predictability may be their first experience of environmental safety in weeks.

2

Apply trauma-aware, relationship-based co-regulation.

Murray and Harrison (2022) show that close teacher relationships are among the strongest predictors of children's adjustment and wellbeing (p. 225).

In practice: For a child from a women-led sole-parent household who has left a domestic violence situation and lost their entire physical environment overnight, the educator relationship may be the only consistency remaining. Offering calm presence without demands, sitting close, and co-regulating before moving to learning follows the sequence Perry's model supports.

3

Protect dignity and avoid forced or pressured disclosure.

Woodrow et al. (2022) emphasise that children and families must not be passive subjects of professional intervention (p. 376).

In practice: For a child in foster or kinship care following a domestic violence removal, disclosure is especially complex; never asking them to explain trauma in a group setting, responding with calm reassurance, and acting on mandatory reporting obligations promptly are all part of the same professional response.

4

Communicate with families warmly and without adding pressure.

Baker et al. (2022) argue that support must capitalise on family strengths rather than focus exclusively on vulnerability (p. 355).

In practice: For a First Nations family displaced by disaster and separated from Country, or for a sole-parent family managing housing crisis after fleeing violence, simplifying communication, offering one consistent contact person, and never raising developmental concerns on the day of disclosure are ethical obligations, not courtesy choices.

5

Facilitate timely, warm, and coordinated intersectoral referral.

Baker et al. (2022) caution that evidence about what works remains limited, making coordinated responses more critical (p. 356).

In practice: Knowing the direct contact of a local domestic violence caseworker, housing support worker, and disaster recovery service before crisis arises, and making the first call with the family rather than leaving them to manage these systems alone, reflect the EYLF V2.0 (AGDE, 2022) requirement for collaborative partnership as core professional practice.
